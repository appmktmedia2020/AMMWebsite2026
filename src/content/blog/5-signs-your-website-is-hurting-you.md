Most small business owners know they need a website. But there's a big difference between having a website and having one that actually works.

A poorly built site doesn't just sit there doing nothing — it actively makes people less likely to call you. Here are five signs it might be time for a serious update.

## 1. It Doesn't Load Properly on a Phone

Over 60% of local searches happen on mobile devices. If your site is hard to navigate on a phone — tiny text, buttons that are too close together, images that break the layout — visitors leave within seconds.

Google also knows this. Mobile usability is a direct ranking factor. A site that fails on mobile loses both customers and search visibility at the same time.

## 2. It Takes More Than 3 Seconds to Load

Attention spans are short. Research consistently shows that the majority of users abandon a site that takes longer than 3 seconds to load. And with local search, a slow site usually means the customer just clicks the next result — your competitor.

Page speed is also a Google ranking signal. Slow sites rank lower, meaning fewer people even have the chance to be frustrated by how long it takes to load.

## 3. It Looks Like It Was Built in 2012

Design trends move fast, and an outdated site signals to visitors — consciously or not — that your business might not be keeping up either. Visitors form an impression of your business within milliseconds of landing on your site.

A clean, modern design doesn't just look better. It builds trust. And in industries where the customer is deciding who to let into their home or hand their money to, trust is everything.

## 4. There's No Clear Call to Action

What do you want visitors to do? Call you? Fill out a contact form? Get a quote?

If your website doesn't make that path obvious and easy, people won't do it. Every page should have a clear next step — a phone number that's easy to find, a button that says "Schedule a Free Estimate," something that moves visitors toward becoming customers.

## 5. You Can't Update It Yourself

If changing your hours, adding a service, or posting a photo requires calling a developer and waiting a week, that's a problem. Your website should be a living, active representation of your business — not a digital brochure frozen in time.
